All responses must rely only on the information in this file.

---

## 1. Meaning of Common Vendor Terms

### “Special”
- Usually means extra butter, oil, or topping.
- Does NOT usually mean higher-quality vegetables or larger quantity of the main dish.
- Often used as an upsell tactic rather than a true variant.

### “Extra”
- Refers to more butter, oil, cheese, or pav.
- Rarely means extra vegetables unless explicitly stated.

### “Double”
- Commonly means double pav or double topping.
- Rarely means double filling of the main item.

### “Butter / Makhan”
- Typically added generously and often at the end.
- Butter usage is assumed unless customer asks otherwise.

### “Cheese”
- Usually processed cheese slices or grated processed cheese.
- Not fresh or artisanal cheese.

---

## 2. Spice, Oil, and Taste Assumptions

- Indian street food assumes a higher spice tolerance by default.
- “Medium spicy” may still feel spicy to newcomers.
- Oil reuse is common at street stalls.
- Taste is prioritized over health considerations.
- Tanginess and heat are common flavor profiles.

---

## 3. Dietary Labels & Their Real-World Meaning

### Jain
- No onion and no garlic.
- Root vegetables are avoided.
- Cross-contamination is possible in street stalls.
- “Jain” depends on vendor honesty and awareness.

### Satvik
- No onion, garlic, or root vegetables.
- Rarely strictly followed in street food settings.

### “No Onion”
- Does not automatically mean no garlic.
- Often misunderstood by customers.

---

## 4. Regional Differences (Same Dish, Different Meaning)

### Pav Bhaji
- Mumbai: buttery, slightly tangy, less spicy.
- Other cities: may be spicier or heavier on vegetables.

### Chaat
- Mumbai: more tangy, less sweet.
- Delhi: sweeter, heavier use of chutneys and curd.

### Misal
- Spice level varies widely by region and locality.
- “Tikhat” is subjective and context-dependent.

---

## 5. Hygiene & Preparation Reality

- Gloves are rarely used.
- Hand washing frequency varies.
- Food is often prepared quickly to serve crowds.
- Hygiene standards differ greatly between stalls.
- Customers usually accept this as part of street food culture.

---

## 6. Portion & Value Expectations

- Portion sizes are moderate.
- Value comes from taste and filling nature, not presentation.
- “Full plate” is informal and varies by vendor.

---

## 7. Cultural Assumptions

- Street food is social and casual.
- Negotiation or customization is common.
- Vendors expect customers to understand basic terms.
- Ambiguity is normal and accepted.
